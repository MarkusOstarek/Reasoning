**Subject:** Scenario-based reasoning discernment test — interest in item validation?

Dear [Prof. van der Linden / Dr. Roozenbeek],

I've built a free, open web tool called Reason Check (https://markusostarek.github.io/Reasoning/) and I'd value your view on whether it could be worth validating properly — your lab's work on inoculation and the MIST is the closest existing research, and part of what motivated the design.

The gap it tries to fill: existing instruments largely test discernment of *false content* (fake headlines, manipulation techniques). Reason Check instead tests reasoning about *true* information — accurate statistics and real events packaged to invite unsound conclusions (hidden denominators, confounded correlations, regression to the mean, selection effects, cherry-picked windows). In my experience this is where much real-world persuasion operates, and it seems underserved.

Design, briefly:

- Realistic everyday vignettes; respondents rate agreement (5-point) with three independently presented readings per scenario: the intuitive-but-unsound one, the sound one, and an overcorrection (blanket cynicism). This yields separate credulity and cynicism signals, MIST-style, rather than a single forced choice.
- Immediate mechanism-naming feedback after each item (active inoculation rather than assessment only).
- Two axes are reported separately and never blended: discernment (agreement with the sound reading minus the flawed one) and overcorrection (agreement with the blanket-doubt reading). Collapsing them would let a credulous and an over-doubting respondent score identically.
- The item bank is fully open (schema, scoring, and sources: https://github.com/MarkusOstarek/Reasoning) under CC BY 4.0, with the code under MIT — adapt, translate or re-field it without asking. An enforced political-valence balance keeps the instrument from reading as partisan, and a second automated audit checks that no surface feature of an item (length, sentence count, punctuation, absolutist vocabulary) predicts which reading is the sound one, so respondents can't pattern-match instead of reasoning.
- Currently 30 draft items across 6 error families; every item cites sources for its empirical claims.

I should be upfront about what the instrument does *not* yet have, since it is the part you'd care most about. There is no validity or reliability evidence of any kind. Three specific limitations I know of: each session draws 12 items from the pool, so respondents do not take identical forms and scores aren't strictly comparable without calibration; the per-family breakdown rests on two items per family, which I now present with explicit uncertainty rather than as a measurement; and the items have never been screened for difficulty or discrimination. I would rather state these than have you find them.

What I'd be interested in exploring, at whatever level suits you: feedback on item construction; whether the two-axis scoring would hold up psychometrically; or, if the tool seems promising, a validation study (I'd be glad to adapt the item bank, instrument, and data collection to your requirements — everything is open and there are no commercial constraints).

On data: the site itself deliberately stores nothing, so I have no response data to offer. If a study went ahead I would expect item-level data to be collected on your side under your ethics approval rather than bolted onto a public site.

Why the limitations paragraph is in there: to a psychometrician, an enthusiast who
hasn't noticed their instrument's weaknesses is a support burden, while someone who
has already catalogued them is a collaborator. It costs nothing — they would find
all three inside five minutes — and it changes which of those two you look like.
