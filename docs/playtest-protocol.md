# Seed playtest protocol

The gate before promotion and before growing the bank. Five to ten people, about
40 minutes each including the conversation.

Everything found so far — surface tells, overclaiming answers, a scenario with
two correct readings — was found by inspection. That method is now exhausted.
The next class of defect (where people quit, what confuses them, whether the
results page lands) is only visible with humans, and no further auditing
substitutes for it.

**Write more scenarios after this, not before.** Ten new items written now would
reproduce whatever this test is about to reveal.

## Who

Diversity of *disposition* matters more than diversity of demographics here.
Aim for at least one of each:

- **The target reader** — smart, not statistical. Someone who'd share a chart.
- **The quiz-hater** — thinks online quizzes are junk. They will find the copy
  that sounds like a personality test.
- **The analytical one** — will argue with a verdict. That is the point; see
  whether their objection is one the bank already answers.
- **Someone whose politics differ from yours.** The balance audit is mechanical
  and cannot tell you whether the thing *feels* even-handed. This is the only
  way to find out, and it is the failure that would hurt most.
- **A phone-only user.** Most social traffic is phones.

Do not recruit only people who will be nice about it.

## How to run it

Send the link with no explanation beyond "would you try this and tell me what
you think?" Any framing you add is framing a stranger from Hacker News won't get.

Watch, in person or over a screenshare, and **stay quiet**. The instinct to
explain is the enemy of the data. If they ask what something means, say "what do
you think it means?" and note that you were asked.

Record, per person:

- Where they hesitated, re-read, or scrolled back up
- Whether they read the explanation after locking in, or skipped to Next
- The scenario number they were on if they quit or flagged
- Anything they said aloud, verbatim, especially complaints
- Whether they finished — and how long it took

## The four questions that matter

Ask afterwards, in this order, without leading:

1. **"What do you think that told you about yourself?"** — tests whether the
   results page communicates anything at all. If they can't answer, the profile
   is decoration.
2. **"Was there any point you nearly stopped?"** — the completion problem,
   located precisely.
3. **"Did any of it feel like it was pushing a view?"** — the credibility
   question. Any hit here outranks everything else in this document.
4. **"Was there an answer you disagreed with?"** — file every one as a dispute
   issue, even if you think they are wrong. Two independent people objecting to
   the same item is a defect in the item regardless of who is right.

## What would change the product

Decide these in advance, so the data can actually overrule you:

| If | Then |
|---|---|
| Under ~60% finish | Do not promote. The funnel is the problem, not the traffic. |
| People skip the explanations | The teaching isn't landing — that is the whole product, not the score. |
| They can't say what the results told them | Cut or rebuild the profile before adding items. |
| Anyone reads it as politically slanted | Stop and fix. This is the failure the project cannot recover from. |
| Two people dispute the same scenario | Treat the item as defective and triage it, whoever is right. |
| Nobody uses the daily mode | Deprioritise it; it is a retention bet that hasn't paid. |
| The six-family breakdown means nothing to them | Collapse to three broader dimensions (causal inference / quantities / social evidence) — 4 items each from the same 12-item test, no extra length. |

## Afterwards

1. File every disagreement as a dispute issue and triage it publicly
   (fixed / pinned / explained / cut).
2. Fix the funnel problems before spending any launch channel. Show HN and
   r/InternetIsBeautiful work once.
3. *Then* grow the bank, informed by which items actually failed.
